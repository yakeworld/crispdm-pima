The Illusion of Predictive Perfection: A Critical Audit of High-Accuracy 
Paradigms and Methodological Leakage in Pima Indian Diabetes Dataset Research
The pursuit of computational diagnostics has placed the Pima Indian Diabetes 
Dataset (PIDD) at the center of a profound methodological tension within the 
machine learning community. Since its publication by Smith et al. in 1988, the 
dataset has served as a primary benchmark for binary classification, originally 
utilized to demonstrate the efficacy of the ADAP learning algorithm in 
forecasting the onset of diabetes mellitus.[1, 2, 3] In recent years, however, a
divergence has emerged between research that adheres to realistic clinical 
benchmarks—typically ranging from 75% to 85% accuracy—and a burgeoning category 
of studies claiming near-perfect classification performance exceeding 95%, and 
in several instances, 99%.[4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9] This disparity does not merely 
reflect advancements in algorithmic complexity but rather highlights deep-seated
methodological flaws, most notably in the form of data leakage and the misuse of
imputation techniques. By critically examining the internal mechanics of these 
high-accuracy frameworks alongside the physiological constraints of the data, a 
clear pattern of "accuracy inflation" becomes visible, necessitated by a failure
to strictly decouple the training process from the target variables and test 
distributions.
